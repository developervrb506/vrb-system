Handicapping Horse Racing Tracks, Post Positions and Conditions



  Tweet

Horses can be a finicky bunch and many excel depending on which post they are awarded


If you’re betting on horse races, your handicapping just can’t stop at looking at the current form of the field. Taking into account the length of the race, the horse’s post position, and the current weather and track conditions can help you find value beyond the racing forum.

Here’s how to work these angles into your racing wagers.

Capping tracks/distance
Some horses are built for speed and others are built for distance. Just look at the Triple Crown races and the challenges they throw at these types of runners. The Kentucky Derby is a 1 1/4 –mile event and benefits those horses who can quickly get out of the gate and keep that pace throughout the race. The Preakness Stakes shortens that distance to 1 3/16-miles and gives the advantage to sprinters, so a slow start is much harder to recover from.

The Belmont Stakes, the third and final jewel of the Triple Crown, is a 1 ½-mile race in which horses that excelled in the Kentucky Derby and Preakness tend to fade down the stretch, giving way to horses with steady speed and endurance. Looking into a horse’s track record at difference distances as well as its lineage (were its sires short or long runners?) is a great way to find hidden value in the odds.

Post positions
Horses can be a finicky bunch and many excel depending on which post they are awarded. Some like to break to the outside and watch the pack, picking their spot to make a move. Others like hugging the rail and are motivated by the close contact of traffic. And other horses aren’t comfortable battling through the middle of the pack, either too timid to make a move or overaggressive and running out of gas too soon. Finding where a horse likes to run and how their post position plays into that is a great way to spot a winner.

Weather/track conditions
Weather can turn on a dime, especially in the spring. A week of rain can leave a track soaked and slick, which can slow down some of the quicker horses in the field. On the other hand, a dry spell can leave a track fast and hard, benefiting those fleet-footed sprinters. And some horses thrive in the muck, and aren’t thrown off by less-than-ideal conditions. It pays off to see which horses excel in those situations and if a bad or good past showing was due to track conditions.
