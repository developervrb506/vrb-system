Red flags horse racing bettors should look out for



  Tweet

These are simple things that can sometimes go overlooked, but should be a red flag when it comes to possibly placing a bet on that particular horse.


When breaking down the daily racing form or handicapping the field for the Kentucky Derby, there are certain things bettors should keep an eye out for that could have a huge impact on your online horse wagering. These are simple things that can sometimes go overlooked, but should be a red flag when it comes to possibly placing a bet on that particular horse. Here are some words of caution:

Track conditions
There are some horses out there that have trained and raced solely on synthetic surfaces. Eventually, these horses will make the switch to natural conditions, on grass or dirt. Synthetic surfaces run much cleaner and faster, so a horse’s times could be skewed due to that quick track. And there’s no way to know how the horse will react to a different surface. Mix in some rain and less-than-ideal conditions and a horse making the switch can be totally thrown of its game.

Starting post/field size
At some tracks, drawing a certain number post can be an immediate death sentence before the race is even run. Knowing how a horse likes to run – push to the outside or shift to the inside – and how their post position plays into that is basic handicapping. But often times a horse that has enjoyed multiple races on the outside or middle of the pack is suddenly thrust to the rail, leaving them rattled from the start.

Another factor that would trigger some alarms is a horse making the jump from smaller fields to bigger fields. These horses have thrived in the open air and haven’t had to battle for position or fight through traffic early on. This challenge can sometime force a horse to run faster than it should or is discouraging and has them letting off.

Jockey switch
Horse racing, like any sport, has its share of politics. Owners or trainers can be forced to pull one rider for another due to off-track conflicts or poor performances, leaving a foreign jockey riding a horse they don’t really know. And there are also a number of injuries in horse racing, with jockeys suffering strains, pulls and sometimes falls. An injured jockey is often times as dangerous as an injured horse in terms of how hard they can push themselves.
