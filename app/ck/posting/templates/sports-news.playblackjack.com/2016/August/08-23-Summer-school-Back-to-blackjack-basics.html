Summer school: Back to blackjack basics



  Tweet

Whether you’re new to blackjack or a long-time player, here are some lessons you should remember the next time you step up to the table


Everybody gets a little lazy during the dog days of summer, so there’s no shame in saying that your once-sharp casino blackjack tips have been dulled a bit by all that fun in the sun. That’s why we’re taking you back to school.


We’re getting back to basics. Whether you’re new to blackjack or a long-time player, here are some lessons you should remember the next time you step up to the table:

Recognizing bust cards
When sizing up the dealer’s hand, there are certain draws that leave them susceptible to busting. Players must go forward with confidence when the dealer is showing these cards and don’t have to chase after cards as much, knowing there’s a good chance the dealer will bust. Players are recommended to stand on counts as low as 14, 15 and 16 in these situations.
According to the percentages, the worst cards a dealer can draw in terms of their bust potential are four (40.28% bust), five (42.89% bust) and six (42.08% bust). Lower cards, like two and three also give the edge to the player, holding bust percentages of 35.30% and 37.56% respectively.

Recognizing bust hands
On the flip side of this lesson, players can end up staring down a near impossible hand from time to time. Recognizing these bust hands and how to play them can save you plenty of sorrow, even if the percentages are against you.
The worst hands the dealer can dish out are in the 15, 16 and 17 range which carry a bust percentage between 58% and 69%. Those hands force the player to really make a call – judging on what the dealer is showing. If the dealer is showing a seven or higher, you have to assume they’re going to turn over a 10-value card and that forces the player to hit in a squeeze.

Recognizing the table
Whether you’re playing one-on-one with the dealer, multiple hands, or among a group of gamblers, blackjack players need to keep an eye on all card showing. Players can simply calculate their chances of drawing a high or low card if they hit by using a simple card-counting technique called High/Low.
Cards two through six are giving a +1 value, seven through nine a zero value, and 10 or higher a -1 value. The player simply tallies the face-up cards and plays accordingly. If the total value lands in the positive, more negative-value cards should be left in the shoe – an edge for the player. A negative sum means more positive-value cards are coming – an edge for the dealer.
