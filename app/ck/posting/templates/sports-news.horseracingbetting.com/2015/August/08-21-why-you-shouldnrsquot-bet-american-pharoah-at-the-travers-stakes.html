Why you shouldn’t bet American Pharoah at the Travers Stakes



  Tweet

The Triple Crown champion is a -400 favorite, with the closest contender paying out at 5/1 , plenty of value on the board.


 

It’s official. American Pharoah is taking his growing legacy to Saratoga for the Travers Stakes on August 29. The Triple Crown champion is a -400 favorite, with the closest contender paying out at 5/1 which means there is plenty of value on the board.

American Pharoah is the most impressive horse the sport has seen in a very long time but the Travers is a tough challenge and there are plenty of reasons to fade him in New York. Here’s a breakdown of why you shouldn’t bet on AP on August 29 from our experts on horse handicapping:

Travel woes
American Pharoah has been racking up the frequent-flyer miles since winning the Triple Crown. He trains in California and jumped the country for his win in the Haskell Invitational at Monmouth Park, New Jersey. He was immediately sent home following that race and now comes back across the nation for this run at Saratoga. American Pharoah proved he was plenty left in the tank at the Haskell but could this busy travel schedule catch up with the three year old?

Graveyard of Champions
Saratoga has a stigma when it comes to elite-level horses. The track has been called the “Graveyard of Champions” with big favorites coming up short of their lofty expectations. Saratoga has been host to upsets over a few legendary horses, with Man ‘o War suffering his lone blemish of a 21-race career, Triple Crown champ Gallant Fox getting beat by a 100-1 long shot by eight lengths, and Secretariat – perhaps the most famous race horse of all time – losing the Whitney Handicap in 1973.

Baffert blunders
Baffert wasn’t high on taking American Pharoah to the Travers in the first place but got his marching orders from owner Ahmed Zayat. The Hall of Fame trainer, who carefully guided AP to the first Triple Crown since 1976, is a dismal 1 for 5 at the Travers Stakes for his career, with his lone win coming from Point Given in 2001. Baffert horses have been dominant since 1999 but are just 8 for 59 in graded stakes races – just a 13 percent winning clip, which pales in comparison to Baffert-trained horses in other events.
